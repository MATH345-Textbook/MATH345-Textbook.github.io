Theorem 5.4.17. Diagonalization Theorem.

Consider a quadratic form \(Q_A\text{,}\) defined in terms of a symmetric matrix \(A\text{.}\) Suppose \(Q\) is an orthogonal matrix such that
\begin{equation*} Q^TAQ=D=\operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n). \end{equation*}
If we set \(\mathbf{x}=Q\mathbf{y}\text{,}\) then
\begin{equation*} Q_A(\mathbf{x})=\lambda_1y_1^2+\cdots+\lambda_ny_n^2.\text{.} \end{equation*}
🔗
🔗

Why is this true?.

Since \(\mathbf{x}=Q\mathbf{y}\text{,}\) we have
\begin{align*} Q_A(\mathbf{x}) \amp = \mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}\\ \amp = (Q\mathbf{y})^TA(Q\mathbf{y})\\ \amp = \mathbf{y}^TQ^TAQ\mathbf{y}\\ \amp = \mathbf{y}^TD\mathbf{y}\\ \amp = \lambda_1y_1^2+\cdots+\lambda_ny_n^2.\text{.} \end{align*}

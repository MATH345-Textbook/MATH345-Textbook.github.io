    Section 3.1 The Cofactor Expansion

    Subsection The Determinant of a General Matrix

    In this section we consider square \(n \times n\) matrices. Our goal is to find a number, called the determinant that we can calculate for any matrix, which tells us whether or not the matrix is invertible.
    Let’s start with the \(2 \times 2\) case. Recall that in Theorem 2.5.7 we defined the determinant of a \(2 \times 2\) matrix
    \begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} a \amp b \\ c \amp d \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    to be \(\det(A) = ad - bc\text{.}\) It has the required properties of the determinant that we will now define for more general matrices.

    Activity 3.1.1.

    Find the determinant of the following matrices:
    (a)
    \begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 1 \\ 4 \amp 2 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} \det(A) = 3(2) - 1(4) = 6 - 4 = 2\text{.} \end{equation*}
    (b)
    \begin{equation*} B = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 2 \\ 6 \amp 4 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} \det(B) = 3(4) - 2(6) = 12 - 12 = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We use the \(2 \times 2\) determinant to define the \(3 \times 3\) determinant. It will not be clear right now why this is the analogous function for \(3 \times 3\) matrices, but we’ll explore that more later.

    Definition 3.1.1. Matrix Minors.

    Let \(A\) be a \(3 \times 3\) matrix. The \((i,j)\) minor of \(A\text{,}\) denoted \(\minor(A)_{ij}\) is the determinant of the \(2 \times 2\) matrix obtained from \(A\) by deleting the \(i\)th row and \(j\)th column.
    In general, if \(A\) is \(n \times n\text{,}\) then the \((i,j)\)th minor of \(A\) is the determinant of the \((n-1) \times (n-1)\) matrix obtained from \(A\) by deleting the \(i\)th row and \(j\)th column.

    Activity 3.1.2.

    Suppose
    \begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ 4 \amp 3 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 2 \amp 1\ \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    (a)
    Calculate \(\minor(A)_{11}\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} \minor(A)_{11} = \det \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 2 \\ 2 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} = 3(1) - 2(2) = -1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    (b)
    Calculate \(\minor(A)_{23}\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} \minor(A)_{23} = \det \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 2 \end{bmatrix} = 1(2) - 2(3) = -4\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Definition 3.1.2. Cofactors of a Matrix.

    Let \(A\) be a \(n \times n\) matrix. The \((i,j)\)-cofactor of \(A\text{,}\) denoted \(\text{cof}(A)_{ij}\text{,}\) is defined as
    \begin{equation*} \text{cof}(A)_{ij} = (-1)^{i+j} \minor(A)_{ij} \end{equation*}

    Activity 3.1.3.

    Define
    \begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ 4 \amp 3 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 2 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    (a)
    Compute \(\text{cof}(A)_{11}\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} \text{cof}(A)_{11} = (-1)^{1 + 1} \minor(A)_{11} = (1)(-1) = -1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    (b)
    Compute \(\text{cof}(A)_{23}\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} \text{cof}(A)_{23} = (-1)^{2+3} \minor(A)_{23} = (-1)(-4) = 4\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Definition 3.1.3. The Determinant of a Matrix.

    Let \(A\) be a \(3 \times 3\) matrix. Then the determinant of \(A\text{,}\) denoted \(\text{det}(A)\text{,}\) is calculated by picking a row (or column), and expanding along that row (column) by taking the product of each entry of that row (column) with it’s cofactor, then adding these products together. The result will be the same regardless of which row or column you choose.
    As an example, if we expand along the \(i\)th row of a matrix \(A\) then we find that
    \begin{equation*} \det(A) = a_{i1} \text{cof}(A)_{i1} + a_{i2} \text{cof}(A)_{i2} + a_{i3} \text{cof}(A)_{i3}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The process of using cofactors to compute determinants is called calculating the determinant by cofactor expansion

    Activity 3.1.4.

    Let
    \begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ 4 \amp 3 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 2 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    (a)
    Find the determinant of \(A\) by expanding along row \(1\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    To calculate the determinant of \(A\) by expanding along row \(1\text{,}\) we write
    \begin{equation*} \det(A) = 1\text{cof}(A)_{11} + 2\text{cof}(A)_{12} + 3\text{cof}(A)_{13}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We begin by calculating the cofactors:
    \begin{equation*} \text{cof}(A)_{11} = (-1)^{1+1}\det\begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 2 \\ 2 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} = (1)(3\cdot 1 - 2\cdot 2) = -1 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{cof}(A)_{12} = (-1)^{1+2}\det\begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} = (-1)(4\cdot 1 - 2\cdot 3) = 2 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{cof}(A)_{13} = (-1)^{1+3}\det\begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp 3 \\ 3 \amp 2 \end{bmatrix} = (1)(8 - 9) = -1 \end{equation*}
    Therefore
    \begin{equation*} \det(A) = 1(-1) + 2(2) + 3(-1) = -1 + 4 - 3 = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    (b)
    Find the determinant of \(A\) by expanding along column \(2\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    To calculate the determinant of \(A\) by expanding along column \(2\text{,}\) we write
    \begin{equation*} \det(A) = 2\text{cof}(A)_{12} + 3\text{cof}(A)_{22} + 2\text{cof}(A)_{32} \end{equation*}
    We calculate the cofactors:
    \begin{equation*} \text{cof}(A)_{12} = (-1)^{1+2}\det\begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} = (-1)(4\cdot 1 - 2\cdot 3) = 2 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{cof}(A)_{22} = (-1)^{2+2}\det\begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 3 \\ 3 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} = (1)(1\cdot 1 - 3\cdot 3) = -8 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{cof}(A)_{32} = (-1)^{3+2}\det\begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 3 \\ 4 \amp 2 \end{bmatrix} = (-1)(2 - 12) = 10\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Therefore
    \begin{equation*} \det(A) = 2(2) + 3(-8) + 2(10) = 4 - 24 + 20 = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Note 3.1.4.

    To track the sign \((-1)^{i+j}\) when computing cofactors, it may be helpful to view the matrix as a checkerboard as below:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} + \amp - \amp + \\ - \amp + \amp - \\ + \amp - \amp + \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    The sign in the \(i\)th row and \(j\)th position with be the sign of \((-1)^{i+j}\text{.}\)

    Note 3.1.5.

    Since the determinant is independent of the row or column you expand to compute a determinant, you should choose the row or column that results in the easiest calculation.

    Activity 3.1.5.

    Compute the determinant of the following matrices:
    (a)
    \begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp-5 \amp 1\\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ -2 \amp -4 \amp 7\end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    The second row of \(A\) has two zeroes, and so expanding along this row will likely result in the simplest computation of the determinant. We write
    \begin{align*} \det(A) \amp= 0\cdot\text{cof}(A)_{21} + 1\cdot\text{cof}(A)_{22} + 0\cdot\text{cof}(A)_{23}\\ \amp = \text{cof}(A)_{22}\\ \amp = (-1)^{2+2} \det \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 1\\ -2 \amp 7\end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = (1)(3 \cdot 7 - 1 \cdot (-2))\\ \amp = 21 + 2 = 23\text{.} \end{align*}
    (b)
    \begin{equation*} B = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp \pi \amp 200\\ 0 \amp 0.03 \amp 4 \\ 0 \amp 19 \amp -3584\end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    If we expand the determinant of \(B\) along it’s first column, we find that
    \begin{equation*} \det(B) = 0\cdot\text{cof}(A)_{11} + 0\cdot\text{cof}(A)_{21} + 0\cdot\text{cof}(A)_{31} = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Note 3.1.6.

    Whenever a row or column of a matrix only has zeroes as entries, expanding along that row shows that the determinant of the matrix is always zero, so no cofactor computations are required.
    Repeating this process allows for the computation of a general determinant.

    Activity 3.1.6.

    Calculate the determinant of the matrix
    \begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4 \\ 5 \amp 4 \amp 2 \amp 3\\ 1 \amp 3 \amp 4 \amp 5 \\ 3 \amp 4 \amp 3 \amp 2\end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Expansion along the third column of \(A\) yields that
    \begin{align*} \det(A) = 3 \amp (-1)^{1+3}\det \left(\begin{bmatrix} 5 \amp 4 \amp 3 \\ 1 \amp 3 \amp 5\\ 3 \amp 4 \amp 2\end{bmatrix}\right)\\ \amp +2(-1)^{2+3}\det\left(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \amp 4\\ 1 \amp 3 \amp 5\\ 3 \amp 4 \amp 2\end{bmatrix}\right)\\ \amp +4(-1)^{3+3}\det\left(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \amp 4\\ 5 \amp4 \amp 3\\ 3 \amp 4 \amp 2\end{bmatrix}\right)\\ \amp+3(-1)^{4+3}\det\left(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \amp 4\\ 5 \amp 4 \amp 3\\ 1 \amp 3 \amp 5\end{bmatrix}\right)=-12 \end{align*}

    Definition 3.1.7. Determinant of a General Square Matrix.

    Let \(A\) be a \(n \times n\) matrix (\(n \geq 3\)). Then the determinant of \(A\text{,}\) denoted \(\det(A)\text{,}\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} \det(A) = \sum_{i = 1}^n a_{ij} \text{cof}(A)_{ij} = \sum_{i = 1}^n a_{ij} \text{cof}(A)_{ij} \end{equation*}
    for some choice of row \(i\text{.}\) Alternatively, one may perform a cofactor expansion along a column \(j\text{.}\)

    Activity 3.1.7.

    Compute \(\det(A)\text{,}\) where
    \begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4 \\ 0 \amp 4 \amp 2 \amp 3\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 2 \amp 5 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp -1\end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Expanding in cofactors along column \(1\) (because most of the entries of that row are zeroes), we find
    \begin{align*} \det(A) \amp = 3 \cdot \text{cof}(A)_{11} + 0 \cdot \text{cof}(A)_{21} + 0 \cdot \text{cof}(A)_{31} + 0 \cdot \text{cof}(A)_{41}\\ \amp = 3 (-1)^{1+1} \det \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ 0 \amp 2 \amp 5 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix} = 3 \det \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ 0 \amp 2 \amp 5 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
    Expanding along the first column again, we find that
    \begin{align*} \det \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ 0 \amp 2 \amp 5 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix} \amp = 4 \det \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 5 \\ 0 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix}\\ \amp = 4 ((2)(-1) - (5)(0)) = -8\text{.} \end{align*}
    So \(\det(A) = (3)(-8) = -24 \text{.}\)
    Recall Definition 2.1.9.

    Definition 3.1.8. Triangular and Diagonal Matrices.

    An \(n \times n\) matrix \(A = [ a_{ij} ]\) is called upper triangular if \(a_{ij} = 0\) for \(i > j\text{,}\) and lower triangular if \(a_{ij} = 0\) for \(i \lt j\text{.}\) A matrix is diagonal if it is both upper triangular and lower triangular, i.e. if \(a_{ij} = 0\) for \(i \neq j\text{.}\) We denote the \(n \times n\) diagonal matrix with entries \(\lambda_1,\dots, \lambda_n\) on the diagonal by \(\text{diag}(\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_n)\text{.}\)

    Theorem 3.1.9.

    If \(A = [a_{ij}]\) is a upper or lower triangular matrix, then \(\det(A) = a_{11} \cdots a_{nn}\text{.}\) That is, the determinant of a triangular matrix is the product of entries in the main diagonal.

    Proof.

    A generalization of the argument used to calculate Activity 3.1.7, i.e. expanding the determinant in cofactors along the first column iteratively.

    Subsection Properties of Determinants

    Theorem 3.1.10.

    If a row or column of a matrix \(A\) consists entirely of zeroes, then \(\det(A) = 0\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    A generalization of the argument used to calculate the determinant in Task 3.1.5.b.

    Theorem 3.1.11.

    If a matrix \(B\) is obtained from a matrix \(A\) by swapping two rows or two columns, then \(\det(B) = - \det(A)\text{.}\)

    Activity 3.1.8.

    Check directly that Theorem 3.1.11 is true when \(A\) and \(B\) are \(2 \times 2\) matrices.

    Theorem 3.1.12.

    If two rows (or columns) of \(A\) are equal to one another, then \(\det(A) = 0\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Let \(B\) be the matrix obtained by swapping the two identical rows / columns of \(A\text{.}\) Then \(A = B\text{,}\) and Theorem 3.1.11 implies that \(\det(A) = - \det(B) = - \det(A)\text{.}\) Therefore \(2 \det(A) = 0\text{,}\) and thus that \(\det(A) = 0\text{.}\)

    Theorem 3.1.13.

    If \(B\) is obtained from \(A\) by multiplying each entry in a row or column of \(A\) by a quantity \(k\text{,}\) then \(\det(B) = k \det(A)\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Suppose that \(B\) is obtained from \(A\) by multiplying each entry of row \(i\) by \(k\text{.}\) Consider a cofactor expansion along row \(i\) of \(B\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{align*} \det(B) \amp = k a_{i1} \text{cof}(B)_{i1} + k a_{i2} \text{cof}(B_{i2}) + \cdots + k a_{in} \text{cof}(B_{in})\\ \amp = k [ a_{i1} \text{cof}(A)_{i1} + \cdots + a_{in} \text{cof}(A)_{in} ]\\ \amp = k \text{det}(A)\text{.} \end{align*}

    Fact 3.1.14.

    If \(A\) is a \(n \times n\) matrix, then \(\det(A) = k^n \det(A)\text{.}\)

    Theorem 3.1.15.

    If \(B\) is obtained by adding \(k\) times the \(i\)th row of \(A\) to the \(j\)th row, for \(i \neq j\text{,}\) which is a row operation which we denote by \(R_j \rightarrow R_j + kR_i\text{,}\) then \(\det(B) = \det(A)\text{.}\)
    We can check Theorem 3.1.15 in the \(2 \times 2\) case. If
    \begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} a \amp b \\ c \amp d \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} B = \begin{bmatrix} a \amp b \\ c + ka \amp d + kb \end{bmatrix}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    then
    \begin{equation*} \det(A) = ad - bc \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{align*} \det(B) \amp = a(d + kb) - b(c + ka)\\ \amp= ad + kab - bc - kab \\ \amp = ad - bc = \det(A) \text{.} \end{align*}

    Theorem 3.1.16. How Row Operations Affect The Determinant.

    Here is a summary of how the elementary row operations, when applied to a matrix, affect the determinant:
    Type I (scaling row/column)
    \(R_i \rightarrow k R_i\) multiplies the determinant by \(k\text{.}\)
    Type II (interchange)
    \(R_i \leftrightarrow R_j\) multiplies the determinant by \(-1\text{.}\)
    Type III (replacement)
    \(R_j \rightarrow R_j + kR_i\) leaves the determinant unchanged.

    Activity 3.1.9.

    Suppose a matrix \(B\) is obtained from a matrix \(A\) by applying the following row operations:
    • \(\displaystyle R_i \leftrightarrow R_j\)
    • \(\displaystyle R_4 \rightarrow 4R_4\)
    • \(\displaystyle R_3 \rightarrow R_3 + 3R_2\)
    If \(\det(A) = 8\text{,}\) what is the determinant of \(B\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    • \(R_i \leftrightarrow R_j\) multiplies the determinant by \(-1\text{.}\)
    • \(R_4 \rightarrow 4R_4\) multiplies the determinant by \(4\text{.}\)
    • \(R_3 \rightarrow R_3 + 3R_2\) does not change the determinant.
    Therefore \(\det(B) = (-1)(4) \det(A) = -32\text{.}\)

    Activity 3.1.10.

    Compute the determinant of the matrix
    \begin{equation*} B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \amp 1 \\ -1 \amp 3 \amp 3 \\ 0 \amp 15 \amp 12 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    We use the elementary row operations to make the matrix \(B\) triangular:
    \begin{equation*} R_2 \rightarrow R_2 + R_1 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 5 \amp 4 \\ 0 \amp 15 \amp 12 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} R_3 \rightarrow R_3 - 3 R_2 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 5 \amp 4 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    None of the row operations we have performed change the determinant of the matrix, and the final matrix has determinant zero, so \(\det(B) = 0\text{.}\)

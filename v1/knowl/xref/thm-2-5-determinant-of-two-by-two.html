Theorem 2.5.7.

The \(2 \times 2\) matrix
\begin{equation*} A=\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
is invertible if and only if \(ad-bc \neq 0\text{,}\) in which case
\begin{equation*} A^{-1}=\dfrac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{bmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
We define the determinant of the matrix \(A\) to be the quantity \(\det(A) = ad - bc\text{.}\) Thus the matrix \(A\) is invertible if and only if it’s determinant is non-vanishing. We’ll learn how to compute inverses for bigger matrices soon.
